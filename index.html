Core Reading List: Dialectical and Historical Materialism in the Indian Context

Here is a streamlined core list of 20 texts. The key moves are: (1) a much stronger economic foundation before the critiques, (2) a mediator (Teltumbde) placed early, (3) the epistemological challenge delayed until you have experienced the method in motion, and (4) a deliberate arc that moves from mastering the logic of capital to negating and expanding it.

Core Reading List: A Dialectical Materialist Path for the Indian Context

Phase A: The Method and the Logic of Capital

(Master the basic movement of dialectical materialism and Marx’s critique of political economy before turning to its limits.)

1. Marx, Karl. “Theses on Feuerbach” (1845).
The eleven theses remain the touchstone. Read them as a compressed manual for a practice-centred materialism. Return to them at the end of every phase.

2. Ollman, Bertell. Dance of the Dialectic: Steps in Marx’s Method (2003), Chapters 1–3.
Ollman teaches the philosophy of internal relations, abstraction, and contradiction with unmatched patience. This is the “how to think” primer. Work through it slowly—it will make everything else possible.

3. Marx, Karl, and Frederick Engels. The German Ideology, Part I: “Feuerbach” (1845–46).
The canonical statement of historical materialism: real individuals, their activity, and material conditions. Pay attention to the claim that consciousness has no independent history; this will be productively challenged later.

4. Marx, Karl. Wage-Labour and Capital (1849) and Value, Price and Profit (1865).
Accessible introductions to the core economic categories—labour-power as a commodity, wages, surplus value. Read them before tackling Capital; they are the scaffolding.

5. Marx, Karl. Capital, Volume I, Parts I, II, and VIII (Chapters 1–6 and 26–33).
This is the biggest change. You will work through the value-form, the fetishism of commodities, the transformation of money into capital, and the historical process of primitive accumulation. Use a companion (Harvey or Heinrich) but stay close to Marx’s own argument. The goal is to trace the dialectical development of the category capital from the commodity to the expropriation of the commons. Only after you can reconstruct this movement in your own words do you move to Phase B.

Phase B: Caste and Gender – The Dialectical Negation

(Now, with the method and the economic logic in your hands, you confront the fact that the “labour” of the value theory is not unmarked. These texts are not add-ons; they restructure what you thought you knew.)

6. Ambedkar, B.R. Annihilation of Caste: The Annotated Critical Edition (Navayana, 2014).
Ambedkar’s 1936 address is an independent theoretical statement on caste as a total system of graded inequality that produces material realities—segregated labour markets, denied water, ritualised violence—that class analysis alone cannot see. Read it now as a negation: does Marx’s “human-sensuous activity” universalise from a subject unmarked by caste?

7. Teltumbde, Anand. Republic of Caste: Thinking Equality in the Time of Neoliberal Hindutva (Navayana, 2018).
This is the mediator. Teltumbde, a Marxist-Ambedkarite, demonstrates that caste is no feudal leftover but a dynamic social relation actively reshaped by neoliberal capital to fragment the working class. He synthesises class and caste within a single analysis, showing you how the internal relation works in practice—exactly the move that prevents “productive tension” from becoming an excuse for never deciding.

8. Vogel, Lise. Marxism and the Oppression of Women: Toward a Unitary Theory (Aakar, 2018 [1983]).
Vogel provides the most rigorous Marxist-feminist account of why the reproduction of labour-power is a structural necessity of capitalist production. This directly challenges the value theory you learned in Phase A and forces a reconstruction: the commodity labour-power has conditions of reproduction that capital simultaneously depends on and fails to value.

9. Rege, Sharmila. Writing Caste, Writing Gender: Reading Dalit Women’s Testimonios (Zubaan, 2006).
Dalit women’s life-narratives are not raw data but a theoretical standpoint. This text asks you to reconsider what “practice” means when the body is treated as polluted, and what “materialism” demands when the sensuous is organised by untouchability. The epistemological challenge introduced here is earned, because you now have a working method to interrogate.

10. Guru, Gopal, and Sundar Sarukkai. The Cracked Mirror: An Indian Debate on Experience and Theory (Oxford University Press, 2012).
This debate drives the point home: does the oppressed have epistemological privilege, or can experience be universalised into theory? You will now be equipped to handle this question without paralysing your analysis, because you have already practiced the method on its own terrain before deconstructing it.

Phase C: Indian Historical Materialism – State, Ecology, and Adivasi Resistance

(Apply the expanded method to India’s past. Caste, class, and ecology are treated not as separate chapters but as internally related.)

11. Desai, A.R. Social Background of Indian Nationalism (1948), selected chapters.
The classic Marxist analysis of the Indian national movement as a bourgeois-led coalition. Read this as a class foundation, and immediately ask how the state this movement produced was simultaneously a Brahminical project—a question Phase B has now made unavoidable.

12. Kosambi, D.D. “The Living and the Dead” and “The Basis of Ancient Indian History” in Exasperating Essays.
Kosambi applies dialectical materialism to ancient India, treating caste as mutable through shifts in productive activity. Read him critically, using Teltumbde’s framework, to identify where technological determinism may eclipse the ideological-reproductive power of Brahmanism. You now have the tools to decide, not just to suspend.

13. Foster, John Bellamy. Marx’s Ecology: Materialism and Nature (Monthly Review Press, 2000), selected chapters on the metabolic rift.
Foster reconstructs Marx’s deep ecological thinking. The concept of the metabolic rift—the rupture in the nutrient cycle between town and country under capitalism—gives you the theoretical ground upon which the agrarian crisis and Adivasi displacement will become legible as ecological contradictions, not just economic ones.

14. Baviskar, Amita. In the Belly of the River: Tribal Conflicts Over Development in the Narmada Valley (Oxford University Press, 1999/2005).
A materialist ethnography of Bhilala Adivasi resistance to the Sardar Sarovar dam. Baviskar shows development as primitive accumulation: the violent separation of people from land, water, and forest. Crucially, she examines how Adivasis get represented as “ecological natives” while the actual material stakes are control over resources. This synthesises ecology with class and coloniality.

Phase D: The Neoliberal Conjuncture – Internal Relations in the Present

(Use the fully developed framework to analyse contemporary India as a structured totality, integrating political economy, gender violence, law, and movement knowledge.)

15. Das, Raju J. Critical Reflections on Economy and Politics in India: A Class Theory Perspective, selected chapters on the informal sector and neoliberalism (Brill, 2019–20).
Das provides a systematic class map of neoliberal India: informal labour is not a residue but a central form of production. This is the rigorous political-economy spine for everything that follows.

16. Menon, Nivedita. Seeing Like a Feminist (Zubaan/Penguin, 2012).
Menon names capitalism as the most salient perpetuator of patriarchy in South Asia. Her structural analysis of marriage, family, and law organises gendered labour without submerging it entirely into class—a dialectical integration you can now critically evaluate, having read Vogel and Rege.

17. Oza, Rupal. Semiotics of Rape: Sexual Subjectivity and Violation in Rural India (Duke University Press/Zubaan, 2023/2024).
Through ethnographic analysis of rape cases in Haryana, Oza demonstrates that sexual violence is not a “cultural” issue separate from political economy but a language through which control over land, labour, and caste hierarchies is contested. This is the concrete, empirical face of a dialectical materialism that refuses to separate violence from accumulation.

18. Kannabiran, Kalpana. Tools of Justice: Non-Discrimination and the Indian Constitution (Routledge, 2012).
A foundational intersectional analysis of constitutional jurisprudence covering caste, tribe, gender, and disability. Kannabiran reveals that law is a contradictory weapon—both site of struggle and instrument of discipline. This equips you to avoid both liberal legalism and crude instrumentalism about the state.

Phase E: Strategic Knowledge from Movements and the World

(Theory must meet the self-generated knowledge of struggles and the global architecture of imperialism.)

19. Sangtin Writers (Anupamlata et al.). Playing with Fire: Feminist Thought and Activism Through Seven Lives in India (Zubaan, 2006/2016).
A collectively-authored text by village-level activists from diverse caste and religious backgrounds. This is not a case study but a theoretical production in its own right. It will force you to confront your own location as a knowledge-producer and ask what it would mean to treat this work as theoretically equal to an academic monograph.

20. Patnaik, Utsa, and Prabhat Patnaik. Capital and Imperialism: Theory, History, and the Present (Monthly Review Press, 2021).
A sweeping historical-materialist analysis of global imperialism. The Patnaiks’ argument for “delinking” from global finance capital forces you to place India’s domestic conjuncture within the world system. The text returns you, finally, to the structural constraints on any socialist strategy—and thus to the strategic question that the entire list was preparing you to answer.

Extended Reading List: Dialectical and Historical Materialism in the Indian Context

Highly recommended to start with the Core list unless you are very familiar with Marxist dialectics.

It combines the original training in dialectical materialism with the new texts on caste, gender, ecology, law, movement praxis, and India’s global political economy.

Phase 1: The Method – Dialectical Materialism as a Way of Thinking

Objective: Break with mechanistic, cause-and-effect materialism. Learn to see reality as a dynamic totality of internal relations, sensuous human activity, and contradiction. Confront from the very beginning the challenge that caste, gender, and lived experience pose to any universalizing method.

  • Marx, Karl. “Theses on Feuerbach” (1845). Marxists Internet Archive. Marx’s eleven short theses are the foundational text for a new materialism centred on practice (praxis). You will learn why “the chief defect of all hitherto existing materialism” is its passive, contemplative stance, and why interpreting the world is inseparable from changing it. This text is your touchstone for the entire reading path.
  • Ollman, Bertell. Dance of the Dialectic: Steps in Marx’s Method (University of Illinois Press, 2003), Chapters 1–3. Ollman is the most patient modern teacher of Marx’s dialectic. He explains the philosophy of internal relations, abstraction, and contradiction with brilliant clarity. Working through these chapters will train you to ask, “How is X internally related to Y?” rather than “What is the impact of X on Y?”—an essential skill for analysing how caste and class constitute each other in India.
  • Ambedkar, B.R. Annihilation of Caste: The Annotated Critical Edition (Navayana, 2014). Ambedkar’s 1936 address is not a supplement to historical materialism but an independent theoretical statement on caste as a total system of graded inequality, ritual violence, and material dispossession. Read it as a demonstration that the social practice of untouchability is itself a form of “sensuous human activity” that produces material realities—segregated labour markets, denied access to water and land, ritualised violence—which class analysis alone cannot see. The text will force you to ask whether Marx’s “human‑sensuous activity” was universalised from a subject unmarked by caste. The annotated Navayana edition distinguishes itself through extensive scholarly notes that track the silences in Marxist treatments of caste.
  • Rege, Sharmila. Writing Caste, Writing Gender: Reading Dalit Women’s Testimonios (Zubaan, 2006). Rege argues that Dalit women’s life-narratives constitute a theoretical standpoint, not mere raw data. This is a method-text: it asks you to grasp how the experience of caste-gender violence restructures what counts as “material” reality. Reading it alongside the Theses on Feuerbach will push you to reconsider what “practice” means when the body is treated as polluted, and what “materialism” demands when the sensuous is organised by untouchability.
  • Guru, Gopal. Experience, Caste, and the Everyday Social (Oxford University Press, 2019). Guru theorises “humiliation” as a structurally produced social relation, not a psychological state. If material life is the starting point of Marx’s method, then the fact that one is socially dead before being exploited for surplus value must be integrated into what “material conditions” mean. This text provides the missing link between the abstraction “labour” and the lived body marked by caste.
  • Guru, Gopal, and Sundar Sarukkai. The Cracked Mirror: An Indian Debate on Experience and Theory (Oxford University Press, 2012). An extended debate on whether the oppressed have an epistemological privilege or whether “experience” can be universalised into theory. It directly challenges the assumption that a neutral, universally valid historical-materialist method is possible when the knowing subject is situated in caste, forcing you to reflect on your own location as you build analytical frameworks.
  • Kosambi, D.D. “The Living and the Dead” and “The Basis of Ancient Indian History” in Exasperating Essays: Exercises in the Dialectical Method. Marxists Internet Archive. Kosambi was one of the first to apply dialectical materialism rigorously to Indian history. These essays show the method in action: he treats caste and the village community not as timeless essences but as mutable forms of productive activity, shaped by technological change and internal class tensions. You see, in a single Indian text, the shift from static fact to dynamic process. Read Kosambi alongside Ambedkar to begin interrogating whether the technological and class drivers Kosambi emphasises can fully account for the ideological-reproductive power of Brahmanism.

Critical Questions for Phase 1

  • Marx says that “the chief defect of all hitherto existing materialism” is its passive, contemplative stance. Does Ambedkar’s analysis of caste reveal a similar defect in a materialism that does not begin from the experience of untouchability?
  • Rege and Guru treat experience as theory. What does adopting a Dalit-woman standpoint do to the category “human” in the phrase “human-sensuous activity”?
  • If theory is always produced from a social location, how should you handle the contradiction between the universality claimed by Marxist method and the partiality of all knowledge? What specific dangers arise for a savarna reader attempting to “apply” these texts?
  • Kosambi treats caste as mutable through shifts in productive activity. Ambedkar insists on the ideological structure of Brahmanism. Is the contradiction between these views a problem to be solved, or a productive tension to be held?

Phase 2: The Framework – Historical Materialism, the Critique of Capitalism, Gender, and Ecology

Objective: Grasp the core of Marx’s analysis of history and the systemic logic of the capitalist mode of production. Understand capital as a social relation, not a thing, and integrate from the start the structural roles of social reproduction and the metabolic relation to nature.

  • Marx, Karl, and Frederick Engels. The German Ideology, Part I: “Feuerbach” (1845–46). Marxists Internet Archive. The canonical statement of historical materialism. You will learn why the real premises are “individuals, their activity and the material conditions under which they live,” and how this method dispenses with speculative philosophy. Pay attention to the argument that morality, religion, and metaphysics have no independent history; they are altered as men alter their material production. This is essential for understanding Hindutva not as an autonomous idea but as a form of consciousness transformed by neoliberal material conditions.
  • Marx, Karl. Wage-Labour and Capital (1849) and Value, Price and Profit (1865). Marxists Internet Archive. These short, accessible works are the best introductions to Marx’s economic thinking. They teach the core categories—labour-power as a commodity, the determination of wages, and the source of profit in surplus labour—without the full complexity of Capital. Reading them first will make Capital far more navigable.
  • Vogel, Lise. Marxism and the Oppression of Women: Toward a Unitary Theory (Aakar Books, 2018 [1983]). Vogel provides the most systematic Marxist-feminist account of why the reproduction of labour-power—mostly done by women as unpaid domestic work—is not external to capital but a structural necessity of capitalist production. Reading this after Wage-Labour and Capital will show you that the commodity labour-power has conditions of reproduction that capitalism simultaneously depends on and fails to value, producing a specific form of gendered oppression that the labour theory of value must accommodate.
  • Marx, Karl. Capital, Volume I, Chapters 1 (§4, “The Fetishism of Commodities”), 15 (“Machinery and Large-Scale Industry”), and 32 (“Historical Tendency of Capitalist Accumulation”). Marxists Internet Archive. These three chapters pack the essential dialectical logic. Chapter 1, §4 reveals how social relations between producers appear as material relations between things—the fetishism that obscures exploitation. Chapter 15 shows that technology is a weapon in the class struggle, directly applicable to platform capitalism in India. Chapter 32 gives you the historical law of motion: primitive accumulation, the centralisation of capital, and the creation of a working class that is “trained, united and organised by the very mechanism of the capitalist process.”
  • Federici, Silvia. Caliban and the Witch: Women, the Body and Primitive Accumulation (Autonomedia, 2004). Federici demonstrates that the historical process Marx calls primitive accumulation was achieved not only through the enclosure of common land but through the violent disciplining of women’s bodies—the witch hunts, the control of reproduction, the imposition of the nuclear family. This text connects directly to Marx’s Chapter 32 and will force you to see that the original separation of producers from the means of production was simultaneously a gendered process, whose logic persists in contemporary enclosures.
  • Foster, John Bellamy. Marx’s Ecology: Materialism and Nature (Monthly Review Press, 2000). Foster reconstructs Marx’s materialist ecology from his notebooks on soil science, forestry, and the metabolic rift between town and country. This is essential for understanding that Marx’s framework is not “Promethean” but contains a deep theory of nature. The concept of the “metabolic rift” will become fundamental when you later analyse India’s agrarian crisis.
  • Harvey, David. A Companion to Marx’s Capital, or Heinrich, Michael. How to Read Marx’s “Capital” (chapters corresponding to the above selection). Harvey and Heinrich are complementary guides. Harvey connects 19th-century categories to 21st-century capitalism with political immediacy; Heinrich is more rigorous on value-form and fetishism. Choose one to walk you through the selected chapters, and consult the other where you need a second angle.

Critical Questions for Phase 2

  • Does the concept of “labour-power” as a commodity adequately capture the work of reproductive labour? What does the invisible reproduction of the worker do to the labour theory of value?
  • If primitive accumulation is a continuous process (as the later Sanyal reading will argue), then is Federici’s analysis of witch hunts simply an historical event, or is the violent regulation of women’s bodies an ongoing mechanism of accumulation in India today?
  • Foster shows that Marx thought ecologically. Why, then, did the Marxist tradition so often become productivist? What methodological moves must you avoid to keep the metabolic relation to nature central?
  • The German Ideology argues that consciousness has no independent history. How, then, can we account for the durability of ideology-forms that structure both capitalism and patriarchy across epochs? Does Vogel’s unitary theory resolve or reproduce this problem?

Phase 3: The Indian Architecture – Foundational Marxist Analyses of Nation, State, Caste, Tribe, and Law

Objective: Apply historical and dialectical materialism to India’s past and post-independence trajectory. Master the work of the scholars who built the Marxist tradition while simultaneously engaging the autonomous Dalit, Adivasi, feminist, and legal-historical analyses that reveal its limits.

  • Desai, A.R. Social Background of Indian Nationalism (1948) and Peasant Struggles in India (1979). Out of print; seek in libraries or used bookstores. Desai is the towering figure of Indian Marxist history-writing. Social Background applies class analysis to the nationalist movement, showing the Indian state as the concentrated expression of a contradictory bourgeois-led coalition. Peasant Struggles demonstrates that the Indian peasantry is not an undifferentiated mass but a modern class in motion, whose revolts are class struggles, not archaic convulsions. You cannot analyse contemporary farmers’ movements without this foundation.
  • Chandra, Bipan, Mridula Mukherjee, and Aditya Mukherjee. India Since Independence (Penguin, 2008), Chapters 1–6. Chandra’s clear Marxist narrative covers the Nehruvian state, the planning era, and the early contradictions. It helps you historicise the present: the tensions between forces and relations of production (the Green Revolution, industrialisation, land reforms) are laid out in a way that directly informs your analysis of today’s agrarian crisis and state-capital alliances.
  • Omvedt, Gail. Dalit Visions: The Anti-Caste Movement and the Construction of an Indian Identity (Orient Longman, 1995). Omvedt traces how the Dalit movement constructed a counter-identity rooted in non-Brahmanical traditions and material struggles over land, labour, and dignity. Read alongside Desai’s class analysis of nationalism, it shows that the nationalist movement was also a struggle over which social identities would constitute the “nation”—a conflict Marxism cannot resolve by appealing solely to class unity.
  • Omvedt, Gail. Buddhism in India: Challenging Brahmanism and Caste (Sage, 2003). A historical study of Buddhism as a 2,500-year anti-caste struggle. Omvedt demonstrates that non-Brahmanical epistemologies have their own rationalist, materialist traditions. Ambedkar’s turn to Navayana Buddhism was not a retreat from materialism but a strategic embrace of an ethical-materialist tradition that could sustain anti-caste praxis. This complicates the assumption that Marxism is the only available materialist method.
  • Kosambi, D.D. An Introduction to the Study of Indian History (1956). Available in reprints. A systematic application of dialectical materialism to the entire subcontinent’s past. It cements the habit of asking how technological change (iron tools, the plough) reshaped the relations of production that we call caste. Use it to see how the “ancient” is actively reinterpreted by historical materialist method. Read it critically alongside Omvedt and Ambedkar to identify where technological determinism may eclipse the ideological-reproductive structure of Brahmanism.
  • Prasad, Pradhan H. Gandhi, Marx and India: An Alternative Path to Progress (Routledge, 2020 reissue), Part I. Prasad offers an internal critique of the post-independence development path from a class perspective, dissecting the contradictions that state-led capitalism failed to resolve. His text forces you to ask: did the “socialist” and Gandhian models resolve underlying class antagonisms, or merely displace them into new crises?
  • Banaji, Jairus. A Marxist Mosaic: Selected Writings 1968–2022 (Haymarket Books, 2024), selected essays on Bombay labour history and the modes-of-production debate. Banaji is a master of integrating theory and history. His essays on Bombay’s working class show capitalist relations emerging in specific, concrete forms; his interventions in the modes-of-production debate teach you to avoid linear stagism and to see Indian capitalism as a complex, uneven articulation of forms of exploitation. This is a methodological model for studying any Indian industrial region today.
  • Baviskar, Amita. In the Belly of the River: Tribal Conflicts Over Development in the Narmada Valley (Oxford University Press, 1999/2005). A classic ethnographic-materialist study of Bhilala Adivasi resistance to displacement by the Sardar Sarovar dam. Baviskar shows that what appears as a “development project” is simultaneously a process of primitive accumulation: land, water, and forest are violently separated from the people who depend on them, with the state mediating this process. Critically, she examines how Adivasis get represented as “ecologically noble savages” while the actual material stakes are land, forest, and water.
  • Gadgil, Madhav, and Ramachandra Guha. Ecology and Equity: The Use and Abuse of Nature in Contemporary India (Penguin, 1995). A systematic analysis of two competing modes of resource use in India: the “omnivores” (industry, urban elites) and “ecosystem people” (Adivasis, forest dwellers, small peasants). This book synthesises political economy with ecological analysis, providing the structural framework for understanding ecological conflict as class and caste warfare.
  • Singha, Radhika. A Despotism of Law: Crime and Justice in Early Colonial India (Oxford University Press, 1998). Before you can analyse the contemporary legal architecture that disciplines labour and caste resistance, you need to understand its colonial roots. Singha shows that criminal law was forged as an instrument of conquest and pacification, directly shaping the coercive institutions the postcolonial state inherited.
  • Nair, Janaki. Women and Law in Colonial India: A Social History (Kali for Women, 2000). A parallel text to Singha, Nair examines how colonial law intervened in gender relations, reconstructing the “Hindu family” and women’s property rights as instruments of governance. The legal production of caste and gender is not a side effect but a core mechanism of colonial domination.
  • Baxi, Upendra. The Future of Human Rights (Oxford University Press, 2002/2012). Baxi, the foremost Indian sociologist of law, distinguishes between “human rights of the globalising classes” and “human rights of the dispossessed,” offering a materialist framework for understanding why law in a liberal democracy can both protect and dispossess. This is essential for grasping the contradictory character of the state-form as it will be wielded in the neoliberal period.
  • Government of India. Towards Equality: Report of the Committee on the Status of Women in India (Ministry of Education and Social Welfare, 1974). This founding document of the modern women’s movement systematically documented discriminatory sociocultural practices and the structural roots of women’s economic marginalisation. Its data on economic participation remains the historical baseline against which India’s current crisis in female workforce participation must be understood.

Critical Questions for Phase 3

  • Kosambi treats caste and the village community as mutable forms of productive activity. How does Omvedt’s account of the Dalit movement’s counter-identity challenge the idea that technology and class alone can dissolve caste?
  • Baviskar shows that Adivasi resistance is framed as “ecological” by outsiders, but the Adivasis themselves are fighting for control over material resources. What does this tell you about the relationship between ecology and political economy in Marxist analysis?
  • Singha and Nair reveal that colonial law actively produced caste and gender categories, rather than simply regulating pre-existing ones. How does this modify the historical-materialist premise that law is a “superstructure” reflecting the economic base?
  • Desai’s class analysis of nationalism gives you a bourgeois-led coalition. From the standpoint of Ambedkar and Omvedt, was the nationalist movement also a Brahmanical project? Can these two determinations coexist in a single totality, and if so, what concept of the state does that demand?

Phase 4: The Contemporary Conjuncture – Political Economy, Caste, Gender, Law, and Ecology in Neoliberal India

Objective: Analyse India’s current capitalism, its informal sector, agrarian crisis, the dialectic of caste and class, the gendered division of labour, the ecological metabolism, and the legal-juridical architecture—all as internal relations within a single, structured totality.

  • Das, Raju J. Critical Reflections on Economy and Politics in India: A Class Theory Perspective, Volumes 1 and 2 (Brill, 2019–20), focus on chapters on the informal sector, neoliberalism, and class politics. Das provides the most systematic class-based framework for contemporary India. He theorises informal labour not as a marginal phenomenon but as a central form of production, applying Marx’s categories of formal and real subsumption. His work equips you with the analytical vocabulary to map India’s class structure as a totality, from corporate capital to gig workers.
  • Teltumbde, Anand. Republic of Caste: Thinking Equality in the Time of Neoliberal Hindutva (Navayana, 2018). This is the essential Marxist-Ambedkarite synthesis. Teltumbde shows that caste is no feudal leftover but a dynamic social relation reshaped by neoliberal capital to fragment the working class and depress wages, while simultaneously giving rise to new Dalit labour movements. His analysis of Hindutva as a political project that uses caste to manufacture consent is directly applicable to post-2014 India.
  • Geetha, V. Bhimrao Ramji Ambedkar and the Question of Socialism in India (Palgrave Macmillan, 2021). Geetha reconstructs Ambedkar’s deep and critical engagement with socialism. You will learn that Ambedkar was not hostile to Marxism but insisted that socialism without the annihilation of caste would reproduce Brahmanical power. This text is crucial for confronting the historical failure of the Indian Left on caste and for building a more dialectical understanding of the class-caste relation.
  • Singh, Hira. Annihilation of Caste in India: Ambedkar, Gandhi, Weber, and Marx (Springer, 2024). The most rigorous recent Marxist theorisation of caste. Singh grounds caste in control over land and relations of production, while simultaneously showing that it has its own historical logic that class analysis must integrate, not reduce. This book helps you move beyond the “caste vs. class” impasse and think in terms of internal relations.
  • Menon, Nivedita. Seeing Like a Feminist (Zubaan/Penguin, 2012). Menon, a leading Indian materialist-feminist, names capitalism as the most salient perpetuator of patriarchy in the South Asian context. Unlike liberal intersectionality frameworks, her approach is structural and power-focused. The book weaves together caste, class, and sexuality, and her analysis of how institutions like marriage, family, and law organise gendered labour is a necessary materialist complement to the Das and Teltumbde readings.
  • Menon, Nivedita. Secularism as Misdirection: Critical Thought from the Global South (Duke University Press, 2024). Shows how the discourse of secularism, far from being a neutral defence against religious majoritarianism, actively hypervisualises women and religion while obscuring the operations of caste, capital, and the state. It argues that secularism often serves capital and anti-minority politics as a strategy of governance. Indispensable for understanding how the Indian state form operates within the current conjuncture.
  • Oza, Rupal. Semiotics of Rape: Sexual Subjectivity and Violation in Rural India (Duke University Press/Zubaan, 2023/2024). Based on eight rape cases in rural Haryana analysed through an ethnographic-linguistic approach, this book demonstrates—with concrete empirical force—that rape is not a “cultural” issue separate from political economy. It reveals rape as a language through which control over land, labour, and caste hierarchies is contested, making it the materialist feminist analogue to your agrarian-political-economy readings.
  • Sangtin Writers (Anupamlata et al.). Playing with Fire: Feminist Thought and Activism Through Seven Lives in India (Zubaan, 2006/2016). A collectively-authored feminist text produced by seven village-level NGO activists from diverse caste and religious backgrounds in rural Uttar Pradesh. This text models the kind of collectively-produced movement knowledge that challenges academic monopoly over theory, and will confront you directly with the epistemological question of who produces knowledge and in what form.
  • Kannabiran, Kalpana. Tools of Justice: Non-Discrimination and the Indian Constitution (Routledge, 2012). A seminal intersectional approach to Indian constitutional jurisprudence covering caste, tribe, religious minorities, women, sexual minorities, and disability. Kannabiran develops a framework for how law addresses—or fails to address—discrimination along intersecting axes. Her chapters on untouchability, Adivasi homelands, and capital punishment ground legal theory in material struggles.
  • Kannabiran, Kalpana. Law, Justice and Human Rights in India: Short Reflections (Orient BlackSwan, 2021). Foreword by Anand Teltumbde. A more accessible entry point covering the Adivasi experience, blocked-by-caste lives, disability rights, minority rights, queer rights, women’s civil liberties, and the death penalty. The foreword by Teltumbde explicitly bridges the caste-class-law nexus.
  • Chatterjee, Partha, and Sobhanlal Datta Gupta, eds. Gramsci in India: Selected Writings 1968–1996 (Orient BlackSwan), editors’ introduction and a sample essay on subaltern consciousness. Gramsci’s concepts of hegemony, passive revolution, and the subaltern are indispensable for understanding how the ruling bloc secures consent. These essays show how these tools apply to Indian democracy, particularly the role of state and civil society in organising bourgeois hegemony over a fragmented working class.
  • Basole, Amit. “The Agrarian Question in India,” and Bhattacharya, Rajesh. “Development of Capitalism in India” in Contemporary Readings in Marxism: A Critical Introduction, ed. Ravi Kumar (Aakar, later edition). These two essays provide concise, sharp analyses of the agrarian crisis (the terms-of-trade squeeze, the fragmentation of holdings, the 2020–21 farm laws as a contradiction between finance capital and petty commodity producers) and the specific character of capitalist development in India. They are perfect entries into the live academic debates.
  • Foster, John Bellamy. Ecology Against Capitalism (Cornerstone, 2003, India edition). Essays applying Foster’s metabolic rift analysis to capitalist agriculture’s depletion of soil fertility, with direct relevance to India’s ongoing agrarian crisis. Read this alongside Basole to give your political-economy analysis an ecological depth it would otherwise lack.
  • Agarwala, Rina. Informal Labor, Formal Politics, and Dignified Discontent in India (Cambridge University Press, 2013). A vital study of how informal workers organise politically, often outside traditional party structures. Agarwala shows that they mobilise around identities and welfare claims, challenging the assumption that class-consciousness automatically emerges from exploitation. This complicates and enriches your understanding of the proletariat today.
  • Sanyal, Kalyan. Rethinking Capitalist Development: Primitive Accumulation, Governmentality and Post-Colonial Capitalism (Routledge, 2007). An original, challenging Marxist theorist. Sanyal argues that post-colonial capitalism structurally produces a “need economy” alongside the accumulation economy, and that the informal sector is not a residue but a product of primitive accumulation reversed through governmentality. Engaging with this text pushes you to ask whether the vast informal proletariat can be assimilated into classical categories without modification.
  • Steur, Luisa. Indigenist Mobilization: Confronting Electoral Communism and Precarious Livelihoods in Post-Reform Kerala (Berghahn, 2017). An ethnographic study of the Adivasi Gothra Maha Sabha (AGMS), whose activists—many with communist backgrounds—broke from the CPI(M) to organise around Adivasi land and identity. It shows neoliberalism fracturing the class project from within and raises the strategic question of identity as a material demand.
  • Shah, Alpa. Nightmarch: Among India’s Revolutionary Guerrillas (Hurst, 2018). Ethnographic account of a 250-kilometre march with a Maoist guerrilla platoon through forested Adivasi villages. It provides the ground-level human reality of the Maoist “people’s war” strategy—its appeal, its contradictions, its internal limits—complicating any purely theoretical verdict on its failure or success.
  • Nair, Manjusha. Undervalued Dissent: Informal Workers’ Politics in India (SUNY Press, 2016). Based on extensive fieldwork in Chhattisgarh, Nair compares two different informal workers’ movements and their strategies. This is a grounded, empirical complement to the theoretical work on the informal sector by Das and Sanyal, and begins to bridge the gap between macro-theory and on-the-ground agency.
  • Emerging concept: Kade, Arati. “Hindutva as Brahminical Neoliberalism.” Look for articles and working papers by this researcher (often circulated in EPW, The Wire, The India Forum). Kade’s framework argues that neoliberal restructuring and Hindutva feed each other: caste serves as an instrument of labour market segmentation, and communal identity offers compensatory power to economically precarious castes. This is cutting-edge work on the 2020s conjuncture.

Critical Questions for Phase 4

  • Menon names capitalism as the most salient perpetuator of patriarchy. Does this submerge gender entirely under capital, or does it preserve gender as a relatively autonomous structure with its own temporality?
  • Oza shows that rape is a language of land and caste conflict. How does this change your understanding of “violence” as a category in historical materialism? Is it merely an epiphenomenon of class struggle, or does it have its own logic that structures accumulation?
  • The Sangtin Writers produce theory collectively and from the ground. What would it mean to treat this kind of text as theoretically equal to an academic monograph such as Das’s? What are the political implications of such a move?
  • Kannabiran’s analysis of constitutional nondiscrimination reveals that law can be a weapon. Yet Upendra Baxi warns of a dual human-rights regime. How do you hold both insights together without collapsing into either liberal legalism or a crude instrumentalism about the state?
  • Steur and Shah both study Adivasi mobilisations that break from orthodox Marxist party formations. Are these movements signs of class recomposition—a new working-class subject being formed—or of the dissolution of a unified class subject? What concept of “class” can contain these identities without reducing them?
  • Sanyal theorises a “need economy” produced by primitive accumulation reversed through governmentality. Does the livelihood struggle of informal workers, as documented by Agarwala and Nair, fit this model, or does it point toward forms of political agency that Sanyal’s framework cannot account for?

Phase 5: Strategic Horizons – Debates on Building Socialism After 1991 and 2014, and Movement Knowledge

Objective: Critically assess the competing strategic theories for socialism in contemporary India, and refine your own capacity for practical-critical analysis by placing theoretical debates in direct dialogue with the lived knowledge of movements.

  • Das, Ritanjan. Neoliberalism and the Transforming Left in India: A Contradictory Manifesto (Routledge, 2018). The definitive study of the CPI(M)’s attempt to navigate neoliberalism in West Bengal. Das analyses how the party’s pursuit of a “creative” parliamentary road led to a contradictory embrace of private capital, using the concept of a “contradictory manifesto” between its ideology and its practice of governmentality. This is an essential case study for any strategic debate.
  • Patnaik, Utsa, and Prabhat Patnaik. Capital and Imperialism: Theory, History, and the Present (Monthly Review Press, 2021). The Patnaiks’ argument for “delinking” is the most influential economic-nationalist path to socialism. They contend that no socialism is possible within globalised finance capitalism and that reclaiming national policy sovereignty to build a domestic pro-people market is the first strategic task. Read this to engage with the theory that directly challenges both neoliberalism and the emphasis on micro-political struggles.
  • Mukherjee, Ananyo. “Governmentality or Class Politics: The Path Ahead for Indian Communists.” Journal of Contemporary Asia, 2024. This recent, high-impact article frames the contemporary strategic choice sharply. It forces you to decide whether the Left should continue within the electoral-governmental paradigm or return to mass, extra-parliamentary class politics. It is an excellent lens through which to evaluate all the frameworks you are reading.
  • Sengupta, Pallab. “The Political Failure of Maoist Line in India: A Call for Rethinking” (Solidnet, 2025). A devastating internal critique of the Maoist “people’s war” strategy from a committed communist. It argues that the Chinese parallel is inapplicable to democratic India and that the line has collapsed in the face of state counter-insurgency and welfare populism. Use it to understand why a purely peasant-based, armed revolutionary path has lost ground.
  • CPI(M) Party Documents on the “Left Democratic Alternative” and M.A. Baby’s recent reflections (e.g., in The Indian Express, 2025). It is essential to read the strategy of the dominant parliamentary left party in its own words. These documents show how the CPI(M) frames a broad anti-BJP secular coalition as a necessary “creative application” of tactics, and why internal critics worry it dilutes class independence.
  • Guha, Ramachandra. “KSSP as Ecological Marxism,” Economic and Political Weekly, 1998; and Zachariah, Mathew, and R. Sooryamoorthy. Science for Social Revolution? Achievements and Dilemmas of a Development Movement: The Kerala Sastra Sahitya Parishad (Zed Books, 1994). The KSSP is a mass people’s science movement that combined Marxist rationalism with Gandhian nonviolent tactics, famously campaigning against the Silent Valley hydroelectric project. Read Guha’s characterisation of KSSP as “ecological Marxism” alongside the detailed study of its achievements and dilemmas. These texts will introduce you to a living strategic experiment that attempted to fuse ecological consciousness, scientific popularisation, and mass mobilisation, and they grapple honestly with its contradictions.
  • Mazdoor Kisan Shakti Sangathan (MKSS) – Campaign documents and pamphlets (1990s–2000s). The MKSS archive—public hearing reports, pamphlets like “Hum Jaanenge, Hum Jiyenge”—documents how a movement of poor workers and peasants turned the demand for access to wage records into a mass campaign that secured the Right to Information Act. Reading these primary documents gives you the texture of how collective agency builds strategic victories, and forces you to assess the relationship between “legal” and “mass” tactics concretely. Key compilations and analyses are available through the National Campaign for People’s Right to Information.
  • Patankar, Bharat, and Gail Omvedt. The Dinkarrao Javalkar Lecture Series: Toilers’ Movements, Freedom Dreams (Shramik Mukti Dal, various years). Patankar’s decades of organising farmer-labour movements in drought-prone Maharashtra—fighting for land, water, and against caste—have produced a body of political reflection that fuses caste annihilation with class politics in practice. These lectures offer a strategic model that is neither party-parliamentary nor Maoist but grounded in mass, non-party political formations.
  • LeftWord Books – Catalogue and Output. LeftWord is the publishing house of the Indian left, with a catalogue spanning classic Marxist texts, contemporary political economy, and movement histories. Unlike academic presses, LeftWord’s list is curated by political actors for political actors. Familiarity with their catalogue positions your reading within the living intellectual infrastructure of the Indian communist movement. Select and read one recent movement history or strategic pamphlet from LeftWord to ground your Phase 5 theorising.

Critical Questions for Phase 5

  • The KSSP fused science, ecology, and mass mobilisation. Did it succeed in building an ecological class consciousness, or did its collaboration with the state blunt its radical edge? What does the KSSP experience tell you about the limits of “ecology” as a unifying frame for class politics?
  • The MKSS turned an information demand into a legal right. Did this empower the working class, or did it channel movement energy into the state’s legal machinery? Does the RTI movement confirm or challenge Mukherjee’s framing of “governmentality vs. class politics”?
  • Patankar’s toilers’ movements operate outside party structures but remain Marxist. Is this a model for a new left strategy, or does it abandon the question of state power?
  • After reading Steur, Shah, and Sengupta together, consider: the Maoist line is declared a political failure by Sengupta. Yet Steur shows Adivasi movements breaking from parliamentary communism, and Shah reveals the lived appeal of the guerrilla struggle. What survives from the people’s war experience—its refusal of electoral democracy, its connection to dispossessed Adivasis—that a new strategy must reckon with?
  • Nair finds informal workers organising around identities and welfare. If these movements build capacity and dignity, but not a classical “class consciousness,” does Marxist strategy need to be redefined? What would a dialectical—rather than a stagist—theory of class formation look like?

Phase 6: India in the World – Foreign Policy, Imperialism, and Global Capitalism

Objective: To develop a dialectical materialist understanding of India’s international relations, analyzing the co-constitution of its domestic political economy (class, caste, state) with its foreign policy. This phase will equip you to critique India’s evolving position within global capitalism, from its anti-colonial leadership in the Non-Aligned Movement to its current role as a sub-imperial power under U.S. hegemony.

1. Foundational Critiques and Global Histories “From Below”

These accessible texts provide a powerful entry point by reframing world history from the perspective of the Global South and offering sharp materialist critiques of India’s contemporary foreign policy.

  • Prashad, Vijay. The Darker Nations: A People’s History of the Third World (The New Press, 2007). The landmark alternative history of the Cold War, told from the perspective of the Global South. Prashad reconstructs the intellectual and political project of the “Third World” as a radical challenge to both Cold War blocs, positioning India’s early foreign policy not as a moral stance but as a strategic articulation of anti-colonial nationalism. This is a vital corrective to Eurocentric IR narratives and provides the essential historical backdrop for understanding the Non-Aligned Movement (NAM).
    • Dialectical Reading: The “Third World” project was an attempt to create an international alliance outside the binary of U.S. and Soviet hegemony. How does this historical moment force you to think about the nation-state as a contradictory form—a tool for anti-colonial resistance that simultaneously consolidates a domestic ruling class?
  • Prashad, Vijay. The Poorer Nations: A Possible History of the Global South (Verso, 2012). This sequel to The Darker Nations analyzes the dismantling of the Third World project through the debt crisis, the rise of neoliberalism, and the fragmentation of the Global South. Prashad offers a sharp analysis of how global financial institutions disciplined postcolonial states, forcing them to abandon the nationalist-developmentalist models that earlier Marxist scholars like A.R. Desai analyzed. This text is crucial for understanding the structural pressures that led to India’s 1991 economic reforms.
    • Dialectical Reading: As you read, trace how the structural adjustment programs of the IMF and World Bank function as a form of imperialism without colonies. How does this external pressure restructure the internal class alliances within countries like India, creating the material basis for a new, pro-globalization elite?
  • D’Rozario, Clifton. “Modi’s Hindu supremacist and pro-US foreign policy have greatly set back regional cooperation.” Europe Solidaire Sans Frontières, 24 Jul. 2024. A concise, politically sharp piece from a CPI(ML) Liberation leader. D’Rozario connects the dots between the Modi government’s domestic Hindutva project, its alignment with U.S. strategic interests, and its deteriorating relations with neighbors. The article concretely illustrates how a “civilizational” foreign policy is rooted in the material interests of an ascendant domestic capitalist class seeking global integration.
    • Dialectical Reading: This piece argues that the BJP’s foreign policy is a direct expression of Hindu supremacist politics. How would you build on this by using the method of internal relations to show how this ideological project is internally related to the needs of Indian monopoly capital seeking new markets and investment opportunities?

2. The Political Economy of India’s Global Integration

These works provide the structural theory to analyze the evolution of India’s foreign policy, directly linking domestic class dynamics to its changing role in the global order.

  • Chakrabarti, Anjan, Anup Kumar Dhar, and Byasdeb Dasgupta. The Indian Economy in Transition: Globalization, Capitalism and Development (Cambridge University Press, 2015). Through a unique Marxist framework, the authors theorize India’s transition to neoliberalism not as a simple policy shift but as a fundamental transformation of the class process. They deploy the concept of the “world of the third” to map how neoliberal globalization dismantled the post-independence economic architecture. This book provides the structural logic for how a shift in accumulation strategy at home (from License Raj to liberalization) forces a reorientation of foreign policy abroad.
    • Dialectical Reading: The book emphasizes “heterogeneity of class processes.” Apply this lens to foreign policy: how does the Indian state, under neoliberalism, come to represent a contradictory coalition of comprador capitalists integrated into global supply chains, a national bourgeoisie seeking protection, and a vast informal proletariat that is both exploited and excluded?
  • Ahmad, Aijaz. In Theory: Classes, Nations, Literatures (Verso, 1992). This modern classic is a foundational text for understanding the relationship between imperialism, nationalism, and cultural production. Ahmad provides a powerful critique of post-structuralist and postcolonial theory, arguing for the enduring centrality of class and imperialism. His concepts of the “peripheral state” and “intermediate classes” offer a sophisticated toolkit for analyzing the Indian state’s contradictory role as both subject and object in the global capitalist system, bridging its postcolonial history with its neoliberal present.
    • Dialectical Reading: Revisit the analysis of the Indian state from your Phase 3 readings (Desai). Use Ahmad’s concept of “intermediate classes” (classes that are neither fully bourgeois nor proletarian) to analyze the social base of the postcolonial state. How does this class configuration explain the historical shift from a Nehruvian foreign policy of “non-alignment” to the current “multi-alignment” under neoliberalism?

3. The Theoretical Core: Imperialism in the 21st Century

These more challenging theoretical texts provide the framework for understanding India’s position within the global capitalist system.

  • Patnaik, Utsa, and Prabhat Patnaik. Capital and Imperialism: Theory, History, and the Present (Monthly Review Press, 2021). A sweeping historical-materialist analysis of 300 years of imperialism, from colonialism to contemporary neoliberalism. The renowned Marxist economists from JNU develop their theory of “asymmetric production capacities” and the “income deflation” imposed on developing countries. This advanced work is essential for understanding the deep structural constraints on national sovereignty in an era of globalized finance capital and for evaluating the “delinking” thesis you would have encountered in Phase 5.
    • Dialectical Reading: The Patnaiks’ theory suggests that the very structure of global capitalism requires a periphery for its stabilization. How does this framework force you to rethink the concept of a “national bourgeoisie” in a country like India? Can it ever be “independent,” or is its structural role always already compromised by its integration into global value and financial chains?
  • Satgar, Vishwas, ed. BRICS and the New American Imperialism (Wits University Press, 2020), Vol. 5 of the Democratic Marxism series. This collection directly challenges mainstream understandings of both U.S. dominance and the BRICS grouping. Through a rigorous Marxist lens, contributors situate the rise of India, China, and other emerging economies not as an automatic challenge to imperialism but as a new terrain of global rivalries. It compels you to interrogate the class character of the BRICS alliance and ask whether India’s role within it represents solidarity with the Global South or complicity in a new “sub-imperial” hierarchy.
    • Dialectical Reading: The BRICS alliance is often seen as a challenge to Western hegemony, yet it includes states with vastly different class characters. How would you use Marx’s method from Capital to analyze the BRICS as a contradictory unity of competing capitals? Is it a counter-hegemonic bloc, or a cartel of rival national capitals seeking a better position within the imperialist system?
  • Karat, Prakash. “Marxism in the 21st Century: Alternative to Neoliberal Capitalism and Imperialism.” The Marxist, vol. XXVII, no. 4, Oct.–Dec. 2011. A fundamental strategic text from a long-time General Secretary of the CPI(M). Karat analyzes the specific challenges that neoliberal imperialism poses for the left, including the role of international finance capital and unilateral U.S. military interventions. This essay offers a first-hand perspective on how a major Indian communist party theorizes and attempts to navigate the global political conjuncture.
    • Dialectical Reading: This text outlines an “alternative to neoliberal capitalism and imperialism.” How does this alternative, proposed by a parliamentary communist party, compare to the strategic debates you examined in Phase 5 (e.g., Patnaik’s “delinking”? Mukherjee’s critique)? Does it address the contradiction of contesting imperialism while governing within a capitalist nation-state?

Critical Questions for Phase 6

  • From “Non-Alignment” to “Multi-Alignment”: How can you explain India’s shift from the Nehruvian policy, which had an anti-imperialist character, to the current “multi-alignment” that includes deep strategic ties with the U.S.? Is this a betrayal of ideals, or a necessary strategic adaptation by an ascendant capitalist class in a new era of U.S. hegemony? How would a dialectical materialist frame this beyond a simple moral judgment?
  • India as a “Sub-Imperial” Power in South Asia: How does a class analysis explain India’s regional hegemony over its smaller neighbors? Is this behavior an anomaly of the Indian state, or is it an expression of the internal logic of a rising capitalist power that requires regional dominance for its economic “development”?
  • The “Internal Relation” of Hindutva and Neoliberal Globalization: Your original list (Phase 4) treats the Hindutva of the Modi regime and its neoliberal economic policies as an internal relation. How does its foreign policy complete this circuit? Does a “Hindu nationalist” ideology serve a specific material function for integrating Indian capital into global markets while disciplining the domestic working class? How does the construction of a “civilizational” enemy (e.g., the “Muslim Other” both domestically and in Pakistan) unify the nation for capitalist expansion?

Where to Begin: Start with Phase 1 and Phase 2 together. Spend a month on Marx’s Theses on Feuerbach, Ollman’s first three chapters, Ambedkar’s Annihilation of Caste, and the selected readings from Capital alongside Vogel. Then move to Kosambi’s essays and the Indian historical architecture of Phase 3, letting the Dalit and Adivasi texts interrupt and restructure the Marxist historiography. Return frequently to the Theses on Feuerbach; they will mean something radically new each time.

Most of the classic texts are freely available on the Marxists Internet Archive. The contemporary academic works are largely accessible through university libraries, LeftWord Books, Navayana, or major database subscriptions. For the most recent articles, consult Economic and Political Weekly (EPW), The India Forum, and NewsClick. Movement documents can be sourced from organisational archives and activist networks.
